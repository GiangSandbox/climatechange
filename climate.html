What's Climate Change?

Climate change is a process which causes long-term changes in temperature and weather patterns. It is putting not just ours, but also the animals' and plants' lives at danger by trapping the heat from the sun and increasing its temperature. Greenhouse gas emissions like Carbon dioxide and methane are the main culprits of climate change, which can be found in a lot of human activities. Ever since the industrial revolution, the temperature has been rising more quickly than ever before. According to an UN report in 2018, Earth is now 1.1°C higher than it was back in the late 1800s and would rise to 2.7°C by the end of the century.

Why did it Happens?

Originally, climate change was only a natural thing, which could only cause small changes in warming. But ever since the industrial revolution, humans have put their hands into climate change, causing it to make more drastic changes. The main reason of climate change process consists of:

  • Transportation (produces massive amounts of COz from burned petroleum-based fuel)
  • Deforestation (Releasing CO2 stored in vegetation and soil annually)
  • Oil & Gas Development (leaks methane out from the operations)
  • Commodity Production (produces things like cement, iron, steel, electricity,
    • plastic, and clothing are made by burning fossil fuels to create energy, which create emissions)
  • Excessive consumption (Our activities contribute to greenhouse gas emissions, like how you use electricity, how you eat, throw away, what you wear, etc.)

[SUMMARY BY NGUYEN TRA MY SANBOX JTC]

Consequences

All causes have consequences, and the consequences to climate change have brought us multiple threats.

The temperature will rise from greenhouse gases. With high temperatures, it can increase heat-related illnesses, difficulty of outdoor activities, and a higher chance of quick-spread wildfires. Global warming heat is mostly absorbed by the ocean. This causes coastal and island communities to melt ice sheets, raise sea levels, and increase ocean acidity, endangering marine life. This has been affecting islands or seaside places like Venice, Italy, which has been at risk of sinking. Heat also evaporates water, causing prolonged droughts. Sand and dust storms, desert expansion, and food scarcity are caused, threatening people's daily water needs and their crops. Other than causing droughts, heat can also cause more intense storms. Leads to severe storms, flooding, and landslides, destroying buildings, houses, and communities, which brings both loss of life and economic loss. Factors that leave people in poverty increase. All the natural disasters have an effect on them, like how flooding sweeps away homes and livelihoods, droughts and water scarcity affect crops, heat causes difficulty in outdoor activities, and more. These disasters displace 23.1 million people in the last decades, leaving many more vulnerable to poverty.

The physical Science

  • Human activities have warmed the atmosphere, oceans, and land, causing rapid and widespread changes to the atmosphere, oceans, cryosphere and biosphere.
  • The scale of recent changes in the climate system has been unprecedented for centuries, even thousands of years. Many changes are irreversible over centuries or even millennia, especially in oceans, ice caps and global sea levels.
  • Man-made climate change affects all regions. There is growing evidence of a link with extreme heat waves, heavy rainfall, droughts, and tropical storms.
  • Global surface temperatures will continue to rise until at least mid-centurv. Unless we make deep cuts in greenhouse gas emissions in the coming decades, if global warming exceeds 1.5 degrees Celsius, then the climate consequences will be even more severe.
  • The hotter the world, the more changes the climate system will have. This includes more frequent and extreme temperature events, heat waves at sea, heavy rainfall, agricultural and ecological droughts in some regions, rates of intense tropical cyclones, and Arctic Sea ice reduction, snow cover and permafrost.
  • Continued global warming will further enhance the global water cycle, making it more variable and altering monsoon rainfall and severity of dry and wet events.
  • As carbon dioxide emissions increase, oceans and land become less efficient at absorbing and slowing the accumulation of carbon dioxide in the atmosphere.
  • With global warming continuing, every region will experience more and more changes in the drivers of climate impacts. Conductors will be more common at 2 degrees Celsius than at 1.5 degrees Celsius, and even more so at higher levels of warming.
  • The collapse of the ice sheets, sudden changes in ocean circulation, and warming beyond currently anticipated are outcomes that are less likely but cannot be ruled out.
  • Limiting anthropogenic global warming requires limiting cumulative carbon dioxide emissions to at least zero. There is also a need to deeply reduce emissions of other greenhouse gases such as methane.
  • Achieving low or very low greenhouse gas emissions will have a significant impact on greenhouse gas and aerosol concentrations as well as air quality within a few years. Notable differences in global surface temperatures will appear in about 20 years.

What happen in COP27?

The UN Climate Change Conference (COP27) took place from 6 - 20 November 2022 in Sharm El-Sheik, Egypt and ended with a breakthrough agreement on a new loss and damage fund for vulnerable countries.

Here is a 10-minute summary of some of the conference's highlights. The UNFCCC secretariat (UN Climate Change) is the United Nations entity tasked with supporting the global response to the threat of climate change. UNFCCC stands for United Nations Framework Convention on Climate Change. The Convention has near universal membership (197 Parties) and is the parent treaty of the 2015 Paris Agreement. The main aim of the Paris Agreement is to keep the global average temperature rise this century as close as possible to 1.5 degrees Celsius above pre-industrial levels. The UNFCCC is also the parent treaty of the 1997 Kyoto Protocol. The ultimate objective of all three agreements under the UNFCCC is to stabilize greenhouse gas concentrations in the atmosphere at a level that will prevent dangerous human interference with the climate system, in a time frame which allows ecosystems to adapt naturally and enables sustainable development.

Vietnam's Response

POSITIVE RESPONSES FROM THE GOVERNMENT

Vietnam is one of the fast-growing economies in Asia, which is going through a far-reaching transformation from an inward-looking planned to one that is globalised and market based. Over the last 25 years, with the policy of “đổi mới” (renovation or new changes), Vietnam has transitioned from an extremely poor country to a lower-middle-income country in less than two decades. Vietnam’s goal is to build solid foundations as an industrialized country by 2020. However, after policies in rural development and poverty reduction, there are still a barrier for Vietnam in the path to become a strongly developing country, which is – Climate change.

At the COP26 climate conference, Vietnam joined the pledge to reduce global methane emissions in 2030 compared to 2010, and made a commitment to reach net zero emissions by the middle of the century. The international community has highly appreciated Vietnam's strong promises and responsible efforts, which have created new prospects for collaboration on low-emission growth, the promotion of the circular economy, and climate change adaptation.

Phạm Văn Tân - Deputy Director of the Department for Climate Change, said in order to fulfill Vietnam’s commitments at COP26, the Government will soon introduce regulations on reducing greenhouse gas emissions and protecting the ozone layer, the national system to track and evaluate efforts to adapt to climate change, a list of the industries and greenhouse gas emitting facilities that must track their emissions, and a strategy for developing Vietnam's carbon market.

By 2050 Vietnam expects:

  • Energy sector emissions will be reduced by 91.6%, and capped at 101 million mtCO2e
  • Agriculture sector emissions will be cut by 63.1%, and capped at 56 million mtCO2e
  • Foresty sector and land use emissions cut by 90%, increase carbon sequestration by 30%, with total emissions and removals reaching at least negative 185 million mtCO2e
  • Waste sector emissions cut by 90.7%, and capped at 8 million mtCO2e
  • Industrial process emissions cut by 84.8%, and capped at 20 million mtCO2e

Furthermore, based on The Prime Minister suggests the concentration on implementing eight contents:
  • The conversion to green and clean energy.
  • Methane emission reduction plan.
  • Methane emission reduction plan and greenhouse gas emissions plan.
  • The reduction of vehicles using fossil fuels and encouragement of vehicles using electricity.
  • Expanding forests to absorb CO2.
  • Producing construction materials and developing green cities.
  • Accelerating digital transformation.
  • Promoting communication to attract the participation of all people and businesses

Each Vietnam’s industry made some specific changes. As for The Ministry of Foreign Affairs and The Ministry of Planning and Investment will will enhance the mobilisation, The Ministry of Agriculture and Rural Development will review the efforts to the reduction of greenhouse gas emissions in agriculture and forestry. The Ministry of Industry and Trade assumes the prime responsibility for reviewing and finalising the National Electricity Development Plan for the 2021-2030 period, with a vision to 2045. The Ministry of Transport will study and develope a roadmap to convert vehicles using clean energy with the cooperation of The Ministry of Construction. Lasly, The Ministry of Science and Technology shall coordinate with the Ministry of Industry and Trade and other ministries and agencies, Study nuclear power development, research new energy sources, formulate climate change standards, and promote technology transfer for adaptation.

Pham Van Tan, Deputy Director of the Department for climate change speaking at the workshop. Source: VNA
As for now, Vietnam is facing a difficulty. Vietnam needs $400 billion from now to 2040 to effectively respond to climate change, according to the Climate Change Department. The State budget can only cover $130 billion, making it a significant financial challenge. To achieve net zero emissions, Vietnam should prioritize investment in infrastructure, improve forecasting, and engage businesses in green transformation, low-carbon technology, and energy efficiency projects. Strengthening international cooperation and mobilizing resources from bilateral and multilateral ties is also crucial. In addition, Vietnam is also trying to stop stop rice warming the planet. Rice, Asia’s principal staple, is to blame for around 10 percent of global emissions of methane, a gas that over two decades traps around 80 times as much heat as carbon dioxide. As one of the world’s biggest rice producer, Vietnam’s government is also trying to stop rice production from making worse effect on climate change.
